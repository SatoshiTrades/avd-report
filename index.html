⚗️ The Fertilizer Price Explosion

Urea — the most widely used nitrogen fertilizer on earth — has nearly doubled in price since January 2026, surging from $350/ton to over $800/ton. The catalyst: US-Israeli airstrikes on Iran on February 28 disrupted the Strait of Hormuz, the artery through which the Middle East's natural gas flows — and natural gas is the primary feedstock for nitrogen fertilizer production worldwide.

UAN28 (urea-ammonium nitrate solution) surged 15% in a single month to $473/ton, and is up 33% year-over-year. Anhydrous ammonia is up 22% since the conflict began. Six of eight major fertilizer types posted month-over-month gains in mid-March 2026. This is not a blip. This is a structural repricing of agricultural inputs that will persist for the duration of the conflict — and possibly beyond.

🇨🇳 China's Export Ban — A Supply Shock With No Equal

In mid-March 2026, China implemented a near-total ban on nitrogen-potassium blends and several phosphate varieties — effectively hoarding its own production while denying the global market access to the world's largest fertilizer producer. China produces approximately 30% of global nitrogen fertilizer and is the largest potash consumer on earth. When China closes its export doors, the global market has nowhere to turn.

This ban is not a tariff adjustment or a quota reduction. It is a hard stop on exports, cited by Beijing as necessary to ensure domestic food security amid geopolitical uncertainty. The cascade effect on global spot prices has been immediate and severe. For AVD — which makes the crop protection and bio-nutritional products that help farmers maximize yield from whatever fertilizer they can afford — this creates an environment of intensifying demand at precisely the moment when planting must happen.

🇮🇳 India's Food Security Emergency — AVD's Hidden Catalyst

India is the second-largest agricultural nation on earth. It has 1.4 billion mouths to feed. Approximately 45% of its crude oil, 60% of its natural gas, and over 90% of its LPG come from the Middle East — and all of it is now at risk. The Indian government invoked emergency powers on March 9, passing a Natural Gas Control Order that throttled industrial gas supply, rationing LPG to residential use only.

India's farmers are heading into the rabi season with fertilizer that is either unaffordable or unavailable. The Indian government is actively seeking US agricultural input supply agreements. American Vanguard, which already operates globally including developing agricultural markets, has a 1,000-product registration footprint across 56 nations. Every emergency procurement drive by India's agricultural ministry is a potential demand catalyst for US-origin crop protection inputs — exactly what AVD produces.

Fifty-four US agricultural groups have already written to President Trump demanding "much-needed market relief" amid surging fuel and fertilizer prices. The policy pressure is building. When it breaks, it breaks toward US domestic producers — and AVD is one of them.

Company Superiority
AVD Is Not a Meme. It's a 57-Year-Old Precision Agriculture Platform.

American Vanguard was founded in 1969. It has been producing, registering, and distributing agricultural chemical and biological solutions for 57 years. It operates in 19 countries through AMVAC, AMGUARD, AgNova (Australia), AMVAC do Brasil, AGRI Center (Latin America), Greenplants (Costa Rica liquid fertilizers), and Agrinos (biological crop inputs acquired for its proprietary high-yield technology platform).

Its current portfolio spans 120 biological solutions globally: 35 specialty nutritionals, 32 biopesticides, 28 biostimulants, and 11 biofertilizers — plus a deep bench of conventional insecticides, herbicides, fungicides, soil fumigants, and growth regulators. Under SIMPAS precision ag technology, AVD can deliver its inputs prescriptively — applying only what is needed, where it is needed, verifiable through the Ultimus MRV system. This is not a speculative startup. It is an operating business with scale, distribution, and regulatory moats.
